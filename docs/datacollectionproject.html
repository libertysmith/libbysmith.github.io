Asian American Pacific-Islanders in the News

Co-Authors: Shivani Chowdury, Sharon Jepkosgei, and Ryan Rynbrandt

Sampling Articles from the New York Times and the Wall Street Journal

A Natural Language Processing (NLP) Text Analysis

If people only learned about AAPI through one news outlet, how would that shape readers’ perceptions of AAPI?

How does that differ between conservative and liberal leaning outlets?

Are there detectible differences Pre- and Post-Covid?

Asian Americans and Pacific Islanders (AAPI) are the fastest-growing ethnic group in the United States, accounting for 7% of the population and growing by 81% between 2000 and 2020 (U.S. Census Bureau, 2022). As with other ethnic minorities, AAPI have faced discrimination and stereotyping throughout American history. Most recently, the COVID-19 pandemic led to an increase in anti-Asian hate crimes in the United States, raising concerns about how AAPI are portrayed in the media. Since media outlets play a powerful role in shaping our views of the world around us, it is important to consider how different media outlets portray AAPI, and how this may influence what people think and believe about them.

Previous research has shown that the partisanship of news outlets can influence their coverage of a variety of topics, including race and ethnicity. For example, one study found that conservative news outlets were more likely to portray African Americans as criminals and liberal news outlets were more likely to portray them as victims of discrimination (Iyengar & Kinder, 1987).

AAPI are often stereotyped in the media as being hardworking, intelligent, and successful. Though such “model minority” stereotypes often highlight positive attributes and achievements, they can be harmful in a variety of ways. Being stereotyped as high-achievers results in the perception of AAPI as having fewer needs than other minority groups or being overlooked for opportunities and support. Thus, the nuanced violence and poverty experienced by AAPI are often left out of the national conversation. Additionally, it can lead to AAPI feeling unnecessary pressure to live up to expectations and to avoid seeking help when they need it. (Sue et al, 2007). As a result of the COVID-19 pandemic, a new iteration of AAPI stereotypes arose. One study analyzed a sample of 1,157 news articles from five major US news outlets and found that AAPI were more likely to be portrayed as dirty, diseased, and dangerous in the media during the pandemic. The study also found that AAPI were more likely to be blamed for the pandemic and to be seen as a threat to public health (Jang et al, 2021).

Our study adds to the existing literature in two important ways. First, whereas previous research looked at the media as a monolithic entity, we seek to explain the influence of media outlets’ partisan leanings in their portrayals of AAPI. Second, we seek to explain the impact of the COVID pandemic in changes in media portrayals of AAPI.

Main research questions:

1. Does the partisanship of national news outlets influence how they portray AAPI?

2. Did this change after the COVID-19 pandemic?

3. To what extent do either liberal or conservative outlets perpetuate stereotypes surrounding AAPI?

This research aims to investigate whether the partisanship of national news outlets influences their portrayal of Asian American Pacific Islanders (AAPI), and whether this portrayal changed after the COVID-19 pandemic. To achieve this, we employ NLP machine learning processes and sentiment analysis techniques using the R and Python programming languages. Our methodology involves the cleaning and parsing of text data collected from one liberal and one conservative news outlet. We extract insights vis-a-vis NLP tools such as Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA), tidytext in R, and the nltk library in Python. This methodology allows us to uncover distinct patterns in language use and sentiment within news articles to investigate the evolution of these patterns over time.

First hypotheses:

H01: News outlet partisanship has no influence on their portrayal of AAPI.

HA1: Conservative and liberal news outlets will portray AAPI differently.

Second hypotheses:

H02: Portrayals of AAPI did not change from pre-pandemic to post-pandemic.

HA2: Portrayals of AAPI changed from pre-pandemic to post-pandemic.

Third hypothesis:

H03: The partisanship of national news outlets does not influence the portrayal of AAPI, and any differences in coverage are due to random variation rather than ideological bias.

HA3: The partisanship of national news outlets influences the portrayal of AAPI, with conservative outlets more likely to perpetuate the model minority myth and liberal outlets more likely to frame AAPI with identity politics and activism.

Population framing and Sample Selection

The data collection process involves the systematic retrieval of news articles published by one liberal and one conservative news outlet, both before and after the COVID-19 pandemic. The articles sampled create a comprehensive corpus for analysis. To test our hypotheses, we collected a corpus of 381 articles about AAPIs from two major U.S. newspapers, the Wall Street Journal (WSJ) and New York Times (NYT) published in 2019 and in 2021 using the ProQuest and LexisNexis Academic databases. Both are globally influential major newspapers of record with massive readership and long-established reputations in reporting and first-hand coverage. Thus, we would expect less divergence in portrayals of AAPI in these sources than we would in a comparison of more extreme and opinionated sources or in a comparison of very different types of outlets. Given the focus on AAPI-related content, we identified a specific set of keywords and phrases related to AAPI, such as ‘Asian American,’ ‘Pacific Islander,’ and ‘AAPI.’ The keyword-based approach ensured the corpus is made of up articles focused on AAPI related issues.

Methodology

Pre-processing steps include tasks such as removing stopwords, punctuation, and irrelevant formatting. The corpus is then tokenized and lemmatized to ensure uniformity and consistency in word representation. We employ Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA), a topic modeling technique, to identify hidden themes or topics within the news articles. LDA works by assuming that each document in our corpus is a mixture of topics, and each topic is a distribution of words. By applying LDA, the topics that most dominate the manner of how AAPI is contextualized are highlighted. In R, we utilize the tidytext package to further dissect the text data in a more granular manner. The tidytext package was instrumental in analyzing bigrams as part of the methodology. It was used to tokenize the text into pairs of consecutive words, allowing for the identification and comparison of frequently occurring bigrams within the articles. Using Python, the nltk library assembled word frequency tables to identify commonly used terms, while word ratio tables compared the relative frequency of these terms between outlets.

One of our primary objectives is to identify words that are disproportionately found in the liberal versus conservative news articles discussing AAPI. To achieve this, we calculate word frequencies within each subject sentence and compare them between the two outlets. We consider words that exhibit significant differences in usage between the outlets as distinctive AAPI context words. These words and phrases serve as indicators of how AAPI issues are framed differently in liberal and conservative news. To assess whether the portrayal of AAPI changed after the COVID-19 pandemic, we compare the usage of distinctive AAPI context words in the pre-pandemic and post-pandemic periods. This analysis provides insights into how the narrative surrounding AAPI issues may have evolved during a critical period in recent history.

Results

Topic Modeling/LDA

The Topic Modeling Analysis provides additional evidence of partisan differences and supports the second hypothesis by illustrating how coverage shifted from pre-pandemic to post-pandemic periods. In 2019, WSJ topics were heavily focused on education and professionalism, with key terms like “admissions,” “students,” and “college” dominating two of its four major topics. This thematic concentration reflects the outlet’s reinforcement of the “model minority” stereotype. In contrast, NYT topics for the same year revolved around political and identity-based themes, with words like “democratic,” “candidates,” and “Andrew Yang” dominating three of its four major topics. This indicates a focus on political narratives and figures tied to AAPI issues. In 2021, both outlets included topics related to hate crimes, such as “violence” and “attacks,” showing a convergence in coverage driven by the pandemic’s impact. Despite this shift, WSJ retained some focus on education-related topics, while NYT expanded its thematic scope to include activism and systemic issues, emphasizing its ideological distinction from WSJ.

The results for the NYT 2019 set of articles making mention of AAPI are above. Not all of the topics are very specific, or clear. The Presidential Campaign of Andrew Yang led much of the discussion surrounding AAPI 2019. This suggests a strong emphasis on identity politics.

The results for the 2019 Wall Street Journal (WSJ) sample are particularly illuminating. Nearly all the identified topics pertain to academic achievement and admissions, with the exception of one topic that captured ProQuest template data. This finding is especially noteworthy in light of the Supreme Court’s decision to strike down college diversity and inclusion policies in a case involving AAPI plaintiffs. These results provide preliminary support for the hypothesis that coverage differs based on partisanship and ideology, at least in the pre-pandemic context. Additionally, they suggest that stereotypes, such as the “model minority” narrative, may be influencing coverage.

Word Ratio Tables

The Word Ratio Tables reveal significant differences in the language used by the Wall Street Journal (WSJ) and the New York Times (NYT), supporting both the first and third hypotheses. Pre-pandemic, WSJ articles disproportionately used words like “students,” “college,” “education,” and “elite,” aligning with the “model minority” stereotype by framing AAPI as academically successful and tied to institutional achievement. For example, these terms appeared with significantly higher frequency in WSJ articles compared to NYT, reinforcing a narrative that emphasizes education and professional success.

Conversely, NYT articles focused on political and social themes, with words such as “voters,” “immigrants,” “political,” and “identity” dominating its coverage of AAPI. These patterns reflect the outlets’ ideological leanings, with WSJ presenting AAPI through a narrow lens tied to success metrics and NYT adopting a broader framing that includes activism and identity politics. Post-pandemic, both outlets began using terms such as “hate crimes” and “violence,” reflecting increased societal focus on anti-AAPI discrimination and attacks. However, WSJ continued to emphasize educational stereotypes, while NYT maintained a more diverse narrative, supporting the notion that conservative outlets are more likely to perpetuate stereotypes than liberal ones.

Bigrams

The Bigram Analysis further substantiates the differences in framing by analyzing common two-word phrases, offering more granular evidence of thematic disparities between WSJ and NYT coverage. Pre-pandemic, WSJ bigrams such as “college admissions,” “high school,” and “affirmative action” demonstrated a dominant focus on education and institutional success, underscoring its reinforcement of the “model minority” stereotype. By contrast, NYT bigrams included phrases like “voting rights,” “ethnic studies,” and “Andrew Yang,” which pointed to a broader engagement with political, social, and identity-based issues. These differences in thematic focus reflect the outlets’ respective ideological leanings, with WSJ concentrating on academic success and NYT addressing systemic and political contexts. Post-pandemic, the bigram analysis revealed more overlap, with phrases like “hate crimes,” “COVID 19,” and “President Biden” appearing frequently in both outlets, reflecting a shift toward shared coverage of pandemic-related issues. However, WSJ’s persistent emphasis on educational themes in pre-pandemic coverage supports the third hypothesis, while NYT’s broader thematic range provides evidence of its more diverse framing of AAPI.

Conclusion

The findings provide robust support for all three alternative hypotheses (HA₁, HA₂, and HA₃). Partisanship significantly influenced the portrayal of AAPI, with WSJ perpetuating the “model minority” stereotype through an emphasis on education and professional success, while NYT framed AAPI issues within broader political and social narratives. Coverage shifted substantially after the pandemic, with both outlets converging on themes of hate crimes and violence while retaining elements of their pre-pandemic focuses. These results demonstrate that partisanship plays a central role in shaping media portrayals of AAPI, and that significant societal events, like the COVID-19 pandemic, can drive shifts in coverage. However, WSJ’s continued emphasis on educational stereotypes highlights the role of conservative outlets in perpetuating static representations of AAPI, while NYT’s broader framing suggests a more dynamic approach to covering AAPI issues.
